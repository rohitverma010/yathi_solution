Bhagavad Gi:tha — Purpose and Structure of the Discourse
Prose transcript (English) of HH Sri Chinna Jeeyar Swamiji's Telugu discourse.
Translated via the prose-first method (translate-timed-telugu skill); the
timestamped subtitle file is gita-purpose-structure.en.srt. The Telugu source
is the only source of truth; Sanskrit terms and quotations are kept
transliterated (and italicized in the SRT), explanations are Swamiji's own.

---

A:pada:m apaharta:ram, da:ta:ram sarva-sampada:m, Lo:ka:bhira:mam Sri:Ra:mam,
bhu:yo bhu:yo nama:myaham. Jai Sri:manna:ra:yana.

Dear devotees — when we set out to begin some task, we ask: why are we doing
it, what is its aim, who is involved, and what must be done. Only after knowing
these do we begin. In the same way, when we take up a scripture to read, we
should know why we should read it, what we should learn, and what benefit it
will bring us. If we feel it will be of use, then we make the chance to read
it, or to do more. The Bhagavad Gi:tha too — for whom, and to reveal what:
knowing this, we should begin it.

Usually everyone keeps saying, "Oh, the Gi:tha is a very great scripture, a
very great scripture." But what is its purpose, and for whom? In the Gi:tha
there is business management, there is personality development, there is
science, there is philosophy, there is theology, and many morals too — this is
there, that is there, so many things, that is how we talk about the Gi:tha. But
when we touch that scripture, if we have clarity about what we must gain and
learn from it, we then try to grasp it from within it.

Now suppose we call the Prime Minister and say, "Sir, sweep the roads a
little." Well, he could sweep the roads too; he may even sweep them. But if
sweeping roads were the purpose, you would not need that man for it — someone
else would do. There are tasks only this person can do. So when receiving such
things — what only he can give — is the main purpose, then we approach him.
That is how we should. Not because we got hold of him, to chatter about this
and that, or to set him to other work — that only shows our own ignorance, our
own incapacity.

Now, when we take up a scripture like the Gi:tha, what is its purpose? Merely
to teach science? For that there are plenty of science books; go to them and
learn all that — you need not get it from the Gi:tha alone. Is it to learn
morals? There are plenty of books for that; go into them and learn those ethics
and rules. Is the Gi:tha meant to teach us some management skills? There are
plenty of management courses; go there and they will explain all the details.
Or to teach rules about food and such? For that there are enough eminent
physicians; through them you can learn and obtain those. So whether the Gi:tha
speaks of such things or not is not the question. But if we know the main aim
of the Gi:tha, then wherever we go and whatever we see, we can recognize whether
it leads us toward that aim — and whether, through it, we are able to attain
that aim. Recognizing this becomes our duty.

What is the main aim of the Gi:tha? Teaching Arjuna his duty is one thing — set
that aside. If He had begun mainly only for that, then once He finished
instructing Arjuna that day, its task would be over; after that it would not be
needed again, and for this age it would be of no use to us either. It would
have remained confined to that one occasion. But the Gi:tha is not a scripture
spoken only for Arjuna, only to spur him to battle.

Then for what was it spoken? For humankind — so that, from when they are born
on this earth until they pass away, knowing what to do makes the tasks they
must do worthy ones. This it must teach: how those tasks should be done, and
how the devotion to duty that all humankind needs should be. Because man had to
be taught this, teaching it is the main purpose of His coming into this world
as Krushna. Thinking so, He took Arjuna as a pretext, an excuse, arranged it
that way, and delivered this through him. This is what we must grasp. The other
things are in it too — Yukta:ha:ra-viha:rasya, "learn to eat rightly";
yukta-svapna:vabo:dhasya, the "early to bed, early to rise" kind of sayings —
those are there. Other rules are not absent either. But teaching those rules is
also meant to lead somewhere: namely, that man must know his duty and act upon
it. For all humanity — to teach this — the Gi:tha came.

That is why the Gi:tha was divided into three parts: the first six chapters,
the second six, the third six — eighteen chapters in all. The first six
chapters came to establish the A:thma, its true nature. These we will not find
elsewhere: go into management courses, you won't learn them; into morals, you
won't know it; into science, even less. Going anywhere else, it is hard to know
it. To learn that, you must come only to the Gi:tha. Coming to the Gi:tha and
saying "I'll seek those other things in it" is folly; and saying "the Gi:tha
omits them" and raising objections against it is equally uncalled for. The
first six-chapter part was named the A:thma Shatkam — it explains the nature of
the A:thma.

The second six chapters were named the Bhakthi Shatkam. Bhakthi means love.
Toward which reality should this love be turned? Toward God. Who is God, how is
He, where is He, what does He do, what is your bond with Him, what must He do
for you, what you long for, the One you take refuge in — and how far is it
possible? All this must be known. To reveal all this, this six-chapter part
sits in the middle of the Gi:tha; the second Shatkam was named the Bhakthi
Shatkam.

After these were spoken, the doubts that arose in Arjuna gave him the courage
to think, "if He explains a little, I can listen and resolve them." After that,
all that was said in the first and second six — twelve chapters in all — was
once put in order and consolidated; and wherever there were some stop-gaps,
some dots needing filling, all those too were added, to tell it completely.
Since these last six chapters came after, they were named the Pu:rva-she:sham —
"the remainder of what was said before." Thus in three parts it was delivered:
an eighteen-chapter scripture, with Arjuna taken as the occasion, instructed by
Sri: Krushna — a most exalted, spiritual scripture, the Bhagavad Gi:tha.

In it, in the first four chapters, He revealed a certain grief, a sorrow that
Arjuna had. For that sorrow to lift, his power of discernment had been
destroyed. Discernment means the capacity to analyze and look — and that
capacity had diminished. What must one analyze? One must discern between the
body and the A:thma. This discerning, they say, is the very first among the
means for a person to improve. Seven means were taught — vive:ka, vimo:ka,
abhya:sa, kriya, anavasa:da, kalya:na, anuddharsha — and of those seven, the
very first is vive:ka, with which He began. Vive:ka does not mean cleverness;
vive:ka means the ability to distinguish things apart — that is its meaning,
discernment.

In what should this discernment be made? Namely: the body and the A:thma are
two different things — one must begin here. How did we come to know this? How
do I get clarity on it? If the food you eat is good, everything is good — your
thinking is what must form in you. For your thinking to be good, first the fuel
put into it must be good; the fuel is the food we eat, so good food must be
taken. So what is good food? By what do we decide the goodness of food? Namely
sa:tvika, ra:jasa, ta:masa — recognizing these categories, we keep away
whatever is unfit and grasp and take whatever is needed. By the influence of
such food, the thinking that arises in you — if you can analyze both this body
and this A:thma and see them — then that is named vive:ka. It begins with what
is called a:ha:ra-suddhi, purity of food.

In our scriptures too, the very first thing said is just this: A:ha:ra-suddhau
sattva-suddhih; sattva-suddhau dhruva: smruthih; smruthi-lambhe
sarva-granthi:na:m vipramo:kshah. First, a:ha:ra-suddhau — if the food is good —
sattva-suddhih, the mind becomes good (sattva means the mind): good food, good
mind. Why should the mind be good? Sattva-suddhau dhruva: smruthih — it is
through the mind that jna:na flows into you, so this flow of jna:na must run
smoothly and unobstructed; no kind of refuse should get into it, that stream,
that current, must be clean and pure. Then from within it the water that is
jna:na flows freely. If that flow runs unbroken, without stopping again and
again, and proceeds steadily, then it is named dhruva: smruthih — a steady,
settled remembrance. If this proceeds rightly, then all the doubts within go
away and all the knots loosen. The direct vision of one's true nature that
should arise, arises. Whatever you must attain, you attain — whatever a human
birth is meant to attain, that you attain. Sarva-granthi:na:m vipramo:kshah —
all the knots of karma that bind you fall away too, and you gain great freedom.
This is what the Upanishad told us.

So — purity of food: the first thing to eat is food itself; without food there
is no life for us. So the food we take must be good — and again, how to decide
that? For that, again, some marks were stated. Ja:thithah — and if someone
somehow contaminates it, those are a:sraya defects; or if something falls in
temporarily and it is defiled, they call it nimitta. Ja:thi, a:sraya, nimitta:t,
adushta:t anna:t — ka:ya-suddhih. This was named vive:ka. That is: things must
not be ja:thi-defiled. Some things by their very nature carry certain kinds of
qualities — some are pungent, some sour, some sweet; this is naturally their
trait. Likewise, some are by nature sa:tvika, some ra:jasa, some ta:masa. Among
those, whichever is sa:tvika for you, those you should carefully select and pick
up; the ja:thi-defiled ones, do not let them come near.

So — ja:thi, a:sraya: whoever handles it, whoever it reaches you through — such
persons' influence too inevitably falls upon that substance at times. So, by
the handling and contact of one without right thinking, without proper
devotion, without proper fitness, one must see that the substance is not
spoiled. A:sraya-adushta:t; ja:thi-adushta:t; a:sraya-adushta:t;
nimitta-adushta:t. Nimitta means something fell into the substance temporarily
— an insect fell in, an ant, a hair, a nail — something falling in temporarily,
or mixing in, or being met with, and the defects that arise from that; this we
call a nimitta defect. That which is not spoiled even by such a nimitta —
ja:thi-adushta:t, a:sraya-adushta:t, nimitta-adushta:t anna:t — taking such food
as fuel and supplying it to the running of this body: ka:ya-suddhih, your body
becomes well and good, one that obeys and does as you say.

If the body obeys, then the mind obeys; if the mind obeys, then from within it
the stream of jna:na flows. If it runs unbroken, the bondage of karma loosens;
and once that bondage is gone, whatever should be seen within is seen. The very
first thing taught here is vive:ka — see that your food is good. Even our
worldly folk said the same: "if there is food, there is muscle, and he with
muscle is the real man." The food must be good. Whatever you desire, it gives:
if you want muscle, muscle; if you want a head, a head; if you want intellect,
intellect; if you want something else, something else. So the food that can
give you whatever you desire is very important. So this was named vive:ka.

He tried to instill this vive:ka in the second chapter. In the third chapter:
with the vive:ka thus gained, what are the deeds you must perform? He named that
Karma Yo:ga. You must do tasks — the body is made for doing tasks, so do them;
but you must know how to do them. All those tasks must be done with awareness,
and that awareness He named jna:na. The tasks refined by this jna:na must be
done — this He said in the third chapter. What kind of awareness? That, again,
is a big question. What should that awareness be like? It must be free of
desire-driven intention. He set a qualification for it: in it you must have no
kind of desire whatsoever, and the intention "this is for me alone" must be
shed. Yasya sarve sama:rambha:h ka:ma-sankalpa-varjita:h — if it is not defiled
by one's desires. Then "I," "mine," "for me" — three dangers: "I am the doer,"
"all this is mine," "it is all because of me" — such notions form in us, do
they not? So if you can keep the work you do in a state freed from these, then
it is like a great Agniho:tra: just as the sacred fire burns up the kindling or
the cotton that falls into it and casts it away, so every task you then do —
sarve sama:rambha:h — whatever the task, by the fire that is jna:na, it becomes
well-refined karma.

If you act knowing this — letting go of desire-driven intentions — do those
tasks freely. Krushna never said to give up the tasks that must be done — but
behind it, He did say to give up what must be given up. What must you give up?
The attachment you have to the action — let that go, and now do your tasks.
What happens then? Like an Agniho:tra, they burn up all your obstacles, cast
them away, and make them vanish. Now begin doing your tasks. These tasks He
named karma; and that awareness which must be kept behind, free of
desire-driven intention, He named jna:na. Now there is karma and there is
jna:na — these two. Karma must be performed; jna:na must be gained within. And
what is gained must become steady; once steady, it must ripen into faith; and
that faith must become full of love. Then these tasks must be done — and then
every task you do brings joy to the one on the other side who receives it. That
tasks should be done this way, He said in the third chapter. God showed us such
a one: He Himself came that day as Krushna, and today, for us, He stands as the
archa:-mu:rthi, in the form of the deity, receiving it in our worship. Thus how
to do the tasks you do, and with what to do them, He told us.

When He said this, the Arjuna who heard it stays only within his own view,
rather than heeding the words of the one speaking — that did not much register
with him. Right then he only wants to pour out his own fuss and his own
feelings, rather than be in the state of "let me also listen a little to what
is being said and reflect on it." Since that state was absent in him, He seems
to be saying tasks must be done, but behind it that awareness is paramount.
"Awareness is paramount, so let me fix my mind on awareness," he thinks. "Then,
work — the more you give it up, the more you reduce it, the more the ease.
So somehow I must give this up; somehow I must depend on jna:na." Now, "there
are two things," you say — yes, there are: work and awareness, karma and jna:na,
these two. "Now how do we manage both?" Well, work must be done — but how? Behind
it there must be powerful jna:na. "So which is paramount?" Jna:na is very
paramount, sir. "Oho — since the wealth of jna:na is what is paramount, then I
need not work."

He listens to His words; they seem to make sense, but since he lacks the
capacity to ponder it and settle it within, he understands it only halfway, and
by what he understood he raises questions. To somehow make it clear to him is
the yearning of Sri: Krushna. The third chapter ended like that. He said jna:na
is very paramount indeed, and one felt one should learn still more about it. So
He explained the whole of the fourth chapter, telling how important jna:na is;
and having said all that, at the end He said, "since you have learned how great
jna:na is, get up and work." "Jna:na is great, yet You tell me to work again? If
jna:na is so great, I would gladly give up everything, sit nicely, and just
meditate like that." But again He said, "uttishtha — uttishtha, Bha:rata!" —
"Arise!" — in the fourth chapter. "So — do you say jna:na, or do you say karma?
Of these two, which exactly is right now, I cannot understand, sir. Will You
make it clear?" As he prays thus, the fifth chapter of the Gi:tha begins.

Let us learn the question; let us learn the answer. Jai Sri:manna:ra:yana.
